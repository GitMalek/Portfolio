About Me Design Projects Other Skills

From The Hearth

A fantasy cooking RPG! My current passion project. You're the newly hired chef for a frontier guild; your meals help your guildmates grow their skills and as people, as you all protect a fragile peace and build something new together.



The Core Loop



You cook tailored meals for a defined cast of characters. They need different types of inspiration based on their roles. the fighter needs martial, the bard needs artistic. Your choice of dish, ingredients, technique, and how you've decorated the guild contrasted with their personal tastes determines what type of inspiration they gain and how much.

The dish you make to cheer someone up should be different from their usual breakfast. Players who pay attention to variety and individual preferences both progress faster, and grow deeper relationships and learn more about their favorite characters.



Design Philosophy



Thoughtful decisions over optimization. There can be no single "best" recipe. A dish's effectiveness depends on who's eating it, what their schedule looks like, time of day, the weather, and their mood.

Context sensitivity. A fighter needs martial inspiration, which they typically get from protein. A character that's going to train soon might benefit more from a hearty egg dish that banks inspiration for them, as opposed to getting an instant kick from seafood.

Social gameplay loop. You don't become the strongest person in the guild, you remain a chef who empowers others. The fighter, medic, bard, and carpenter grow stronger and unlock new capabilities, and everyone relies on each other.

Core Systems

If every dish just provided generic buffs, players would optimize to a single recipe and repeat it endlessly. How do you make a reward system where the "best" dish changes based on who you're cooking for and what they're generally doing? How do I incentivize variety and thoughtfulness rather than repetition?

Buffs and flat bonuses can still exist, but the core incentive for cooking has to be one that ties into every other aspect of the game, being context sensitive on a character-by-character basis AND other external circumstances, such as schedule.



Inspiration Economy



Inspiration types: General, Culinary, Martial, Artistic, Medicinal, Crafting

Progression is structured around inspiration, a resource similar to Runescape's skill-specific experience points. Different characters need different types- martial for the fighter, artistic for the bard. Most food generate pools of "General" inspiration by default, and ingredients convert portions of that pool into specific types during cooking. Inspiration is spent on level-ups, perks, abilities, and purchasing items from characters like the carpenter.

To add depth beyond "feed Martial-based food to the fighter," inspiration has three delivery methods:

Characters with a faith could unlock a perk to convert divine inspiration to another type at 2:1. A cleric character is planned for the future that can improve it.

To keep the system forgiving rather than punishing, I added manual conversion. If a character gains an inspiration type they don't really use, you can convert it in a menu at a cost (3:1 for specific types, 2:1 for general inspiration, as opposed to the 1:1 during the cooking process). This makes suboptimal cooking feel recoverable rather than wasted- maintaining the warm and cozy atmosphere, but also rewarding and encouraging thoughtfulness.

The option to do this is available at any time, so the player doesn't have to think about it until they need inspiration of a type they don't have. If a character's short of something they want to spend it on, they can just convert a type they can convert from a type they don't use often then and there. All inspiration is good, some is just more valuable for certain characters. There's room to introduce some perks that lower the conversion rates between two types down the line.



Notation Crafting



Most cooking systems are pure menu selection or execution minigames. Neither captures the thoughtfulness I'm trying to evoke from cooking- considering what ingredients you have, how to prepare them, and who they're for.

Effects are assigned at different levels of a hierarchy that defines what an ingredient 'is'. Each category can carry its own 'modifiers' that decide what the final item does. An ingredient is first defined by it's primary food group, like proteins. The group decides the list of available sub-categories, like meats or eggs... which then decide type, which decides form. Give it a try below to see how changing one 'element' of an ingredient affects it as a whole!

Whole Chicken Egg

I got the idea for this system when I read this paper on parametric cooking . I call it notation-based crafting, because while the player never has to see it- I'm actually structuring ingredients like chemical formulas entirely on the backend. This is exclusively for my convenience, because it makes it easier to keep track of what makes up a complex ingredient, and also easily design recipes that can accept multiple parameters by writing out their notation. This means no matter how detailed a player decides to make their cooking (whether they want to use a specific type of wheat to make flour for a cake dough, or simply uses something premade) the final dish will hold the modifiers of everything used to make it.



Modifiers



The notation and inspiration systems create meaningful choices, but they're complex. How do I add depth for players who want to optimize without overwhelming those who just want to cook decent food? To me, the answer was modifiers. They follow a hierarchy of abstraction in which simple, high level decisions are the most important- with the hyper-specific modifiers being the most niche and only necessary for slight optimization, and deeper roleplay.

Modifiers attach to an 'element' of each food item (group, subgroup, type, form), and are structured into the following tiers of importance:

Core modifiers are unavoidable—you engage with them just by picking ingredients. A player thinking "proteins for the fighter" is already using the system decently well.

Cereal's best served in the morning, tea tastes better on rainy days, sweets can help break someone out of a sad mood.

Supplementary modifiers reward attention to taste profiles (Some characters have a preference for what tastes are dominant between savory, sweet, sour, bitter and spicy) and context like time of day, mood and weather. Ignoring them won't halt progression, but engaging with them grants a worthwhile bonus both to inspiration and developing relationships.

Specialization modifiers are edge cases- form preferences (luxury food, runny eggs, etc.), cultural origin (some recipes carry modifiers that note them as signature dishes of places in the setting. Depending on a character's view of the nation, they may love or hate it!), perk bonuses (e.g., Picking up a baking perk that makes your baked goods increase relationship point gain). Optional depth for players who want to optimize or roleplay a specific chef identity. They typically focus entirely on relationship points which aren't necessary to progress.

The more specific your choices, the narrower the effect. Players can start with the food group; "proteins for the fighter" then learn "eggs before training, meat when she doesn't have an over-time buff active." Everything beyond that incrementally rewards deeper thinking, without punishing those yet to engage.

If you'd like to recontextualize the ingredients we went over by looking at their modifiers, feel free to use the ingredient explorer below!

Whole Chicken Egg
A 'base ingredient' that can't be cooked is written like this: P1(T1F1).

P means it's a protein, the first subgroup in the list of proteins is 'Egg', so P1.

T1 just means 'first type in the list of eggs', which is chicken. F1 is the same, but for form, which is whole. So this is one whole chicken egg. A 'complex ingredient' is written out like this, Frg1(Ht1(P1(T1F1)))(T1F1).

The only two new elements are Frg1 and Ht1.

In the notations, cooking methods are 2 letters, complex ingredients are three.

This means you're looking at a chicken egg with Ht1, heating 1, or 'frying' applied to it. It's a Frg, a Fried EgG.

If there's optional ingredients, they're included in the notation separated by a +, so Ht1(P1(T1F1) + P1(T1F1)) is the fried egg recipe, with an optional extra egg that's been added in, both correspond to the same recipe, but the notation stores the extra info! It's easy to make recipes that accept all sorts of different items. For example, veggie stir-fry can be Ht1(V*), which means that as long as it's fried, any vegetable is valid! A fried egg is Ht1(P1(T*F*)), as long as it's an egg that's fried- it accepts any type or form!

Don't worry, all of this notation business is purely backend, it just makes recipe creation and validation easy as pie. The player never has to see it.

Progression

The core systems detail the most basic version of inspiration gain. You unlock new recipes, combine better ingredients and carry over their stronger modifiers into the final dish. Still, the incremental increases aren't enough to evoke the thoughtful and meaningful progression we're after.

NPCs need to feel like they're contributing to the growth of the guild and the survival of the settlement, not just eating food for a bigger number. The player also needs to progress in a way that doesn't overshadow the characters they're meant to lift up.

Player Progression



The player can eat and grow too! They gain culinary inspiration when they cook, and it funds two types of progression:

Skill Unlocks: These are permanent improvements- new recipes, cooking method-specific modifiers, additional optional or additive ingredient slots. These are straight forward power increases.

Chef Identities: These are specializations that sometimes come with trade-offs! They work a bit like Disco Elysium's thought cabinet, you can 'equip' these identities once you've unlocked them.

Patisserie
+ Buff on all cake dishes
Vegetarian
+ More Protein stacks on fungi
+ More Inspiration from vegetables
− Less Inspiration from meats
Aphrosi Cuisine
+ Buff on all recipes tagged Signature Dish: Aphros
+ Buff on dishes with an equal amount of Sweet and Sour, or Bitter and Sweet modifiers
− Less inspiration on all recipes tagged Signature Dish: Eudaina


Horizontal Vs. Linear



If you'll notice, progression on the player's side is mostly horizontal. The fighter and medic follow linear paths- you help them become better at their jobs, they evolve on their own terms. The bard and carpenter work closer to you, so they offer choices- which songs to unlock, which furniture to prioritize.

NPC progression unlocks new verbs, not just bigger numbers. The fighter opens your ingredient list, the medic manages risk, the bard amplifies your cooking, the carpenter provides permanent upgrades. You empower them, they empower you.

Narrative Design

The Stardew Valley style player-centric calendar with freeform progression works well for a cozy atmosphere, but it wouldn't portray the other characters as ones with their own lives, nor as ones you're relying on as much as they're relying on you. I needed a structure that gradually introduces the complexity of the system while maintaining narrative momentum and stakes, and also allowing for failure without frustration.



Structure



While I still want to maintain a calendar system, I've opted to have the narrative revolve around story arcs. A good comparison is the Persona games that still utilize their calendar system, but build up towards a deadline in which the player must overcome a gameplay challenge relevant to the current arc in the story.

An arc like a raid on the settlement would provide you with structured goals. You would need your fighter and medic at certain levels to overcome it, for example. Still, I had some issues with it. You either had to design those challenges as being very easily beatable unless you're trying to fail (like in Persona), or they would be frustrating. I opted to think of a way to have the player fail forward, and what I came up with was a time loop narrative structure.



Introduction



The game's tutorial is the guildmaster teaching you to fry an egg. Most players will just pick the recipe, press "cook" and move on, making a basic hard fried egg. The guildmaster will approve, though mentioning they happen to personally like over-easy eggs. They'll give the player another egg, and inform them that if they press up then down while cooking certain meals, they can flip them, and if they press space, they can end cooking early in the visible timing windows.

The day ends, but the night is interrupted. An assassin attacks, the fighter is taken by surprise and is unable to save you. You loop back to the tutorial. The guildmaster doesn't seem to immediately believe the outlandish story if you opt to tell it then and there, but says they'll keep it in mind.

Now the player can apply their knowledge: if they cook the over-easy egg immediately, they gain bonus relationship points and unique dialogue. That night, your warning thwarts the assassination, the guildmaster and fighter believe you since you've demonstrated impossible knowledge. This teaches the core loop in the first five minutes. Knowledge persists across loops, and paying attention to preferences matters.

Also, as a useful easter egg, if someone starts a new save and cooks an over-easy egg from the get-go despite not being told how to, they're given a dialogue option to skip this first loop- they've clearly gone through it already!



Progression



The goal is to make failed attempts diagnostic rather than punishing. The first loop sets a precedent that sometimes you might have to fail to know how to proceed. If you over-level the fighter but neglect the medic, you stop the raid but the assassin's poison kills the fighter anyway. The loop resets, you discuss with guildmates, they give you information about what you need to prioritize.

Your culinary inspiration and recipe knowledge persist, so each loop makes you stronger while teaching you the systems' interdependencies. Players who struggle spend more loops building culinary progression, naturally catching up to those comfortable enough with the system to end up on the correct path the first time around intuitively.

As a developer-side benefit to this structure, the game's first arc can release as a vertical slice with future arcs dropping in content updates. Since the player character is the key to the time loop, it makes sense they'd be restricted to just a mess hall for the first arc!



Character Arcs



These story arcs are character-focused. Players should feel like their cooking, dialogue and leveling decisions affect characters' growth, but I can't write entirely separate storylines for every possible outcome. The solution is to design character arcs around a core philosophical tension that maps to mechanical and dialogue decisions players are already making. The arc's conclusion reflects those accumulated choices through small but meaningful splits that provide a mechanical and narrative difference persisting across loops.



Example: Rosetta's Arc

Rosetta, the Fighter

Rosetta is the fighter, a noble from Eudaina (a nation valuing arts and magic) who funded the settlement expedition but doesn't quite fit aristocratic life. Her family possesses a "Generational Arte," a unique inherited magic made stronger by being known by as few as possible. Rosetta has poor control over hers. Currently she can only summon a single sword.

The first arc's assassin uses the appearance of Rosetta's childhood friend, catching her off guard during the attack. Through investigation, players discover her friend is actually leading the raiders. He cut ties with Rosetta years ago as his family's wealth deteriorated, eventually becoming a thief who resents nobility.

Should Rosetta embrace her inheritance to protect those she cares about, or make an effort to succeed on personal strength and tenacity alone?

The player influences Rosetta's path through cooking choices and leveling decisions. Feeding her lavish meals triggers dialogue where she questions whether it's acceptable to indulge in noble comforts. Your response adds points towards one outcome. How heavily you level Rosetta affects the amount of points for either outcome in the final battle.

Inheritance Path: Rosetta believes that refusing her advantages doesn't help anyone. In the final confrontation, she properly wields her arte, summoning and commanding additional swords. This makes Rosetta more powerful mechanically, but in a future arc where her friend is redeemable and recruitable as another fighter, he's weaker and obtaining him is more challenging. This also gives her a preference for lavish meals.

Tenacity Path: Rosetta refuses to rely on her inherited power, proving herself through hard-won skill. This requires significantly higher leveling to succeed in the raid. Rosetta doesn't gain any buff from her Arte going forwards, but when her friend is redeemed and recruited, he becomes more powerful and the two gain synergy bonuses when working together. This also gives her a distaste for lavish meals.

The variation is meaningful but contained. Both paths maintain Rosetta's core characterization and reach the same narrative beats. The difference is how she reaches those beats and what strengths she develops. Players feel agency because their cooking and leveling choices shaped her growth, but I'm not writing two entirely separate storylines.



Worldbuilding



I design nations, factions, and cultures around internal tensions that create natural story hooks and character conflicts. When building a faction, I start with a core tension or duality that makes perfect adherence to its ideals impossible. This creates space for complex characters who struggle with their culture's contradictions rather than simply representing it. These nations also tie into the cooking system through cuisine bonuses and character preferences.

Example: Aphros

Taipan, Focus of the Second Arc, an Aphrosi Lawyer

Aphros is a theocracy built around duality. Their faith holds that good must learn from evil to triumph, and that a complete life requires experiencing both sin and virtue. Of course, they acknowledge that one must ultimately be virtuous, but leaving important tools such as lies and deceit only in the hands of the cruel is to cede a whole life to only them. This faith manifests in their governance through two political factions, Astra & Umbra: Astra handles diplomacy, public culture, and holy warfare, while Umbra manages espionage, sanctioned occultism, and clandestine operations. Both acknowledge each other's roles openly. While publicly, Astra is meant to keep Umbrans from straying too far into sin- and Umbra is meant to seek the necessary evils Astrans may be too careful to attempt... both parties cooperate quite extensively. Astrans can be just as conniving as Umbrans, and Umbrans as kind as Astrans.

Their cultural obsession with duality extends to their cuisine. Aphrosi dishes favor contrasting flavor combinations like sweet and sour or bitter and sweet. Characters from Aphros prefer dishes marked with the "lavish" modifier and alcoholic beverages, as they're staples of the masquerade balls they use to train their spies. The Aphrosi Cuisine chef identity buffs recipes from Aphros while weakening ones from their bordering nation, Eudaina- which is becoming sick with the amount of international incidents that prop up between the two. Aphrosi characters also typically incorporate black and white into their designs!

This worldbuilding creates plot hooks without extra work. The first arc's assassin is a Faceless, a shapeshifting race originating from Aphros. A later arc introduces Aphrosi characters building a nearby settlement, bringing political intrigue and opportunities to cook for guests with specific cultural preferences. Every aspect of the game, from the world, to the characters, to the writing and mechanics are meant to coalesce into creating as interesting a cooking fantasy as possible.
